\begin{abstract} 
% For solving large-scale inconsistent systems of linear equations of tensors under the t-product, we propose  (i) tensor greedy double  block extended Kaczmarz, and (ii) tensor greedy double  block averaged  extended Kaczmarz. We prove theoretically  that each method converges linearly in expectation to the least-squares minimum-norm solution of the tensor equation. Numerical experiments  conducted show that the  tensor greedy double block extended Kaczmarz and the tensor greedy double block averaged extended Kaczmarz  can be more effective  than  the tensor randomized extended Kaczmarz, the tensor randomized extended average block Kaczmarz and the tensor, and the tensor randomized extended greedy block Kaczmarz algorithm.

The randomized Kaczmarz method is a widely adopted iterative method for solving  linear systems of equations. To solve large-scale inconsistent linear systems of tensor equations under the t-product, we propose a tensor greedy double block extended Kaczmarz (TGDBEK) method. We prove  theoretically the convergence guarantees and showed that the proposed method converges linearly to the minimum-norm least-square solution of the tensor system. Moreover, we assess the performance of the proposed method through several numerical experiments. Compared to existing methods, the TGDBEK does not require predefined partitions of the tensor system and reduces the running time for solving large inconsistent system and requires less iteration.

\vspace{.5mm} 

{\bf Keywords}. Tensor equations, randomized extended Kaczmarz, tensor greedy randomized Kaczmarz,  t-product.

\section{Introduction}
In this work, we focus on solving the tensor inconsistent  linear system  of the form 
\begin{align}
\label{eq:eq1}
\mathcal{A} * \mathcal{X} =  \mathcal{B},\;  \fm B = \overline{\fm B} + \epsilon
\end{align}
where $\mathcal{A}\in\mathbb{R}^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3},\;
\mathcal{B}\in\mathbb{R}^{N_1\times K\times N_3},\;
\mathcal{X}\in\mathbb{R}^{N_2\times K\times N_3},$  are third-order tensors, $\epsilon$ the contamination,  and $*$ the t-product proposed by Kilmer and collaborators  \cite{kilmer2011facto, kilmer2013thirdOrder}. The t-product \comment{for tensors} provides a matrix-like algebra for third-order tensors, including analogues of inverse, transpose, orthogonal projections, and  pseudo-inverse, etc. \cite{kilmer2011facto, kilmer2013thirdOrder}. One can see \cite{zhang2018, qi2021, Wang2020} for applications of the t-product in various topics like Singular Value Decomposition, Image recognition, neural network \cite{wang2020}. The problem eq.\eqref{eq:eq1} arises in many applications such color image recovery,  MRI images deblurring with multiple channels or multiple slices, where the tensor  $\fm X$ is the unknown clean tensor image to be found, $\fm A$, is a blur operator tensor, and $\fm B$ is the observed blurred and noisy data. When the noise is relatively higher, it becomes difficult to solve this problem  through direct methods. Moreover, this setting makes typically an inconsistent system  where the problem is a least-square problem and a natural targeted solution is the minimum-norm  solution $\fm A^\dagger * \fm X$. \comment{ in the t-product sense. }

In the matrix framework where $\fm A$ is a matrix and $\fm X$ and $\fm B$ are vectors,  iterative methods like the randomized Kaczmarz (RK) method can be  used to solve  the linear system  eq.~\eqref{eq:eq1}.
% $A x = b$. 
% \cite{strohmer2009, needell2009randomized, needell2010randomized} can be applied to   to solve equation of the form \eqref{eq:eq-1}. 
The RK method selects rows  at each iteration with probability proportional to their Euclidean norm square. It was proved that this row selection strategy  makes the RK to converge linearly in expectation  for consistent and over determined linear systems \cite{strohmer2009}.  To solve inconsistent linear systems, Zouzias and Freris \cite{zouzias2013rando} enhanced the randomized Kaczmarz by introducing an additional iterative dual variable and proposed  the randomized extended Kaczmarz (REK) which  converges linearly in expectation to  the minimum-norm least square solution $A^\dagger x$, where $A^\dagger $ is the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse of the system matrix $A$. 
% The innovation in the REK is to doubly apply the randomized Kaczmarz method to both rows and columns. In other words, for the REK method \cite{zouzias2013randomized}, the iteration consists of two parts as follows
% \begin{align*}
%     z^{k+1} & = z^{k} - \frac{A_{:, j}^\top z^k}{\Vert A_{:, j}\Vert_2^2}\\
%     x^{k+1} & = x^k - \frac{A_{i, :}x^k - z^k_i}{\Vert A_{i, :}\Vert_2^2}
% \end{align*}
It is worth mentioning that the RK method can be slow or can reduce to the cyclic Kaczmarz method when the matrix  coefficients are scaled  in an efficiently way. % by a diagonal matrix. 
Two practical ideas emerged and were studied in order to speed up the randomized Kaczmarz method. The first modifies the  row sampling by introducing block sampling and results to block variants randomized Kaczmarz (and  other related averaging variants) \cite{necoara2019faste, du2020rando,tondji2023faster}. In other words, in order to sample a single row, block-based randomized Kaczmarz methods and their variants sample a bock of rows with probability proportional to their Frobenius norm square. The second observes that the rows or blocks can be selected in each iteration according to some greedy  criterion  where only rows or blocks with largest residuals are selected. This  results to greedy randomized Kaczmarz  variants studied in \cite{bai2018greed,bai2019greed,bai2021greed} and references therein . The greedy selection shrinks the rows or blocks candidates  list to project on and typically  can results in a fewer iterations and convergence decay. 


% The Randomized extended Kaczmarz 
% The  RK method or its block variants selects a row  or a block of rows at random and projects  onto its solution hyperplane, yielding a linear convergence rate in expectation \cite{tondji2023faster, needell2009randomized, needell2010randomized}. 
% Several variants of the Randomized Kaczmarz method were proposed to both solve consistent and inconsistent linear systems \cite{zouzias2013randomized}

% The randomized extended Kaczmarz (REK) \cite{zouzias2013randomized} was introduced to solve inconsistent linear systems. It introduces an auxiliary update variable that drives the residual toward $\ker A^T$ and was proved  to converge linearly in expectation to the a least-square solution. The row selection strategy of the RK or REK can results to uniform sampling of the rows  when the matrix entries are scaled in a convenient way and results to slow linear convergence. Two  practical accelerations strategies have been studied: block sampling \cite{necoara2019faster, needell2009randomized} ( and its averaging/ relaxation variants \cite{necoara2019faster, du2020randomized}) and greedy selecton rules \cite{bai2018greedy, niu2020greedy}. The block-based randomized kacmarz method samples block of rows instead of a single row and results in a faster convergence rate \cite{necoara2019faster, needell2009randomized}, wherease the greedy-based Kaczmarz variants constraints the selection of rows on those having larger residual residuals. This strategy  cuts down the required running time and oftern requires less iteration than the vanilla Kaczmarz.  These ideas have been recently  extended to the t-product framework \cite{TREK}

These ideas have been recently brought to the t-product tensor setting. In their seminal work, Ma and Molitor \cite{molitor2022} proposed  the tensor randomized Kaczmarz (TRK) method , which extends the RK method in matrix form to solve tensor linear systems of equations under the t-product. It was shown that using the t-product linear algebra that the TRK method  is equivalent to the block randomized Kaczmarz method in the Fourier domain and hence it is superior compared to the  RK  method applied to vectorized tensor system. Subsequently, Chen and Qin\cite{chen2021} utilized the randomized Kaczmarz method to address tensor recovery problems and proposed the randomized regularized Kaczmarz method. Numerical experiments demonstrated that the Kaczmarz method performs well in various signal/image recovery and image striping tasks. For applications of the randomized Kaczmarz method in tensor recovery and completion problems, please refer to reference \cite{chen2021,wang2022, wang2023, lia2024, zhang2024}. The authors of \cite{ba02020TRB} introduced the tensor randomized block Kaczmarz (TRBK) method, which enhances accuracy beyond the TRK method. These methods are all proposed based on the assumption of equation consistency. Huang et al. \cite{huang2023TREK} proposed a tensor randomized extended block Kaczmarz method (TREBK) to solve inconsistent equation, which breaks the constraint of equation consistency and can obtain a high-precision solution. The TREK method requires computing a pseudoinverse at each iteration. Following that observation, recently,  Liyuan An and collaborators \cite{lian2024TREABK} proposed a pseudoinverse-free randomized extended block Kaczmarz that  leverage the averaging idea  we have seen in \cite{necoara2019faste, du2020rando}. Numerical experiments have shown that the TREABK method is relatively similar to the TREBK. 

% It is worth noting that many 
Many of these tensors block methods we have seen above \cite{lian2024TREABK, ba02020TRB} rely inherently on predefined partitions of  rows or columns of the tensor system, which can be problem-dependent and many interact unfavorably with heterogeneous sparsity patterns commons in practical problems.  In this paper, inspired by the \cite{su2025}, we apply the concept of the greedy randomized block  extended Kaczmarz method
from matrices to tensor equations. Consequently,  we propose a tensor greedy double block extended Kaczmarz (TGDBEK) method, which dynamically builds active set for row and column slices at each iteration, avoiding fixed partitions and focusing  projection computation where the  the current residual is the largest. In addition, we establish the convergence guarantes of the TGDBEK. Finally, we also demonstrate practical effectiveness of the proposed method against the TREBK, TREGBK \cite{huang2023TREK} and TREABK \cite{su2025}. We also highlight its application on image deblurring problem and on sparse  tensor system constructed from  SuiteSparse matrices \cite{suiteSparse}.

The remainder of the paper is organized  as follows.  Section \ref{sec:prelim} introduces some definitions and notations that will be used in the sequel. In  Section \ref{sec:tdgbek}, we describe the TGDBEK  algorithm and give the convergence guarantee.  Numerical examples and application are provided to discuss the effectiveness of the proposed method in Section \ref{sec:numexp}. Finally, some conclusions are drawn in section \ref{sec:concl}.

   
\section{Notations and preliminaries} \label{sec:prelim}
In this section , we discuss about the notations and the properties of tensor using the t-product that are  used in the sequel.
\subsection{Basic notation}
Throughout the paper,  the calligraphic letters such $\fm A, \fm B$ are used  to denote tensors. For an integer $m\geq 1$, we denote $[m]:=\{1, \ldots, m \}$. For a three-order tensor $\fm A$  $\fm A_{ijk}$ denotes the $(i, j,k)^th$ element of $\fm A$. $\fm A(i, :, :)$, $\fm A(:, j, :)$ and $\fm A(:, :, k)$ are used to denote the horizontal slices, the lateral slices, and the frontal slices of the tensor $\fm A$, respectively. The pseudo-inverse of $\fm A$ is denoted as $\fm A^\dagger$ and the minimum and the maximum non zero singular values of a matrix are denoted as $\sigma_{\min}(\cdot)$ and $\sigma_{\max}(\cdot)$, respectively.


\subsection{Tensor preliminaries}
In this part, we provide  a brief review of main  definitions and results related to tensor using the t-product that are used in the sequel.  We use the notations from [][].\\
For a third-order tensor $\fm A\in \r^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$, let $\m A_i = \fm A(:, :, i)$. We define the block circulant matrix  bcirc($\fm A$)  of $\fm A$ as ,
$$
    \bcirc(\mcala):=\bem\mbf A_1 &\mbf A_{N_3} & \cdots & \mbf A_2\\ \mbf A_2 & \mbf A_1 & \cdots &\mbf A_3\\ \vdots &\vdots & \ddots &\vdots\\ \mbf A_{N_3}& \mbf A_{N_3-1}& \cdots &\mbf A_1\eem\in\mbbr^{N_1N_3\times N_2N_3}.
$$
Moreover, we define the operations $\fold(\cdot)$  and its inverse unfold($\cdot$) as follows
$$
\unfold(\mcala):=\bem\mbf A_1\\ \mbf A_2\\ \vdots\\ \mbf A_{N_3}\eem\in\mbbr^{N_1N_3\times N_2},\qquad \fold\left(\bem\mbf A_1\\ \mbf A_2\\ \vdots\\ \mbf A_{N_3}\eem\right):=\mcala.
$$ 

\begin{definition}[t-product, \cite{bai2019partially}]
For $\mcala\in\mbbr^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$ and $\mcalb\in\mbbr^{N_2\times K\times N_3}$, the {\it t-product} between $\mcala$ and $\mcalb $ is  a $ N_1\times K\times N_3$ tensor  defined and denoted  $\mcala*\mcalb$ 
$$
\mcala*\mcalb:=\fold(\bcirc(\mcala)\unfold(\mcalb)).
$$	
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[\cite{an2024randomized}]
Let $\mcala \in \r^{N_1\times \times N_2 \times N_3}$ and $\mcalb \in \r^{N_2\times K\times N_3}$. Then the t-product between $\fm A $ and $\fm B$ satisfy the following properties
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The separability in the first dimension, that is 
    \begin{align*}
        \left( \fm A * \fm B\right)(i, :, :) = \fm A(i, :, :) * \fm B.
    \end{align*}
    \item  The component form decomposition on the second dimension
    \begin{align}
        \fm A * \fm B = \sum_{j=1}^{N_2} \fm A(:, j, :) * \fm B (j, :, :).
    \end{align}
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[identity tensor]
 The identity tensor $\mcali\in\mbbr^{N\times N\times N_3}$ is the tensor whose first frontal slice is the $N\times N$ identity matrix, and whose other frontal slices are all zeros.	
\end{definition}
For $\mcala\in\mbbr^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$ and $\mcali\in\mbbr^{N_1\times N_1\times N_3}$, it holds that \beq\label{ii}\mcala_{i,:,:}=\mcali_{i,:,:}*\mcala.\eeq For $\mcala\in\mbbr^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$ and $\mcali\in\mbbr^{N_2\times N_2\times N_3}$, it holds that \beq\label{ij}\mcala_{:,j,:}=\mcala*\mcali_{:,j,:}.\eeq

\begin{definition}[transpose]
The transpose of $\mcala\in\mbbr^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$, denoted by $\mcala^\top$, is the $N_2\times N_1\times N_3$ tensor obtained by transposing each of the frontal slices and then reversing the order of transposed frontal slices $2$ through $N_3$.
\end{definition} 
For $\mcala\in\mbbr^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$, we have  \beq\label{ttop}\bcirc(\mcala^\top)=(\bcirc(\mcala))^\top.\eeq %and \beq\label{eqj}\mcala*\mcala^\top=\sum_{j=1}^{N_2}\mcala_{:,j,:}*(\mcala_{:,j,:})^\top.\eeq 
   
\begin{definition}[identity tensor]
 The identity tensor $\mcali\in\mbbr^{N\times N\times N_3}$ is the tensor whose first frontal slice is the $N\times N$ identity matrix, and whose other frontal slices are all zeros.	
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[inner product]
The inner product between $\mcala$ and $\mcalb$ in $\mbbr^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$ is defined as $$\la\mcala,\mcalb\ra:=\sum_{i,j,k}\mcala_{ijk}\mcalb_{ijk}.$$ 
\end{definition}

For $\mcala\in\mbbr^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$, $\mcalb\in\mbbr^{N_2\times K\times N_3}$, and $\mcalc\in\mbbr^{N_1\times K\times N_3}$, it holds that \beq\label{tran}\la\mcala*\mcalb,\mcalc\ra=\la\mcalb,\mcala^\top*\mcalc\ra.\eeq

We will intensively employ the Frobenius norm of tensors to measure the distance between tensors. We  provide the spectral norm, Frobenius norms under the t-product.
\begin{definition}  For $\fm A \in \r^{N_1 \times N_2 \times N_3}$, its spectral norm and the Frobenius norm are defined as follows
\begin{align}
    \Vert \fm A \Vert_2 &  := \Vert \bcirc (\fm A ) \Vert_2\\
    \Vert \fm A \Vert_F &:= \sqrt{\langle \fm A , \fm A \rangle} = \sqrt{\sum_{i,j,k}}\fm A(i,j,k)^2
\end{align}
\end{definition}
\begin{lemma}
    For $\fm A\in \r^{N_1\times N_2 \times N_3}$ and $\fm B\in \r^{N_1\times N_2 \times N_3}$, it holds that 
    \begin{align}
        \Vert \fm A * \fm B \Vert_F\leq \Vert \fm A \Vert_2 \Vert \fm B \Vert_F
    \end{align}
\end{lemma}
\begin{definition} For $\fm A\in \r^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$, its pseudoinverse, denoted by $\fm A^\dagger$, is the $N_2\times N_1\times N_3$ tensor satisfying
\begin{align}
    \bcirc(\fm A^\dagger) = \bcirc(\fm A )^\dagger
\end{align}
\end{definition}
\begin{lemma} For $\fm A\in \r^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$ and  $\fm B\in \r^{N_2\times K\times N_3}$, the following holds
\begin{align}
\fm A^\top * \fm A * \fm A^\dagger = \fm A^\top * \fm B
\end{align}
\end{lemma}
\begin{definition}[\cite{an2024randomized}] For $\fm A\in \r^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$, the range  and the null of of $\fm A$ are defined as 
\begin{align*}
    \text{range}(\fm A ) &:= \left\{\fm A * \fm Y\; :\; \fm Y\in \r^{N_2\times K\times N_3} \right\}\\
    \text{null}(\fm A) &:= \left\{ \fm X \in \r^{N_2\times K \times N_3 }\;:\; \fm A * \fm X = 0    \right\}
\end{align*}
\end{definition}
\begin{lemma}
For $\fm A\in \r^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$ and $\fm X\in $ range($\fm A$),  the following holds 
\begin{align}
    \Vert \fm A^\top * \fm X \Vert_F^2 &  \geq \sigma_{\min}^2(\bcirc(\fm A))\Vert \fm X \Vert_F^2
\end{align}
\end{lemma}

\section{Numerical experiments} \label{sec:numexp}
In this section, we assess the  performance of  the TGDBEK method through numerical experiments to demonstrate its effectiveness compared with TREABK \cite{}, TREBK\cite{}, TREBK\cite{}, and TREGBK. We use  PyTorch \cite{} to implement and run our esperiments. All the experiments were conducted in Python and PyTorch on  Google Colab and a Macbook m3 Apple chip.\\
We measure the performance of the aforementioned methods using the number of iteration steps ("IT") and the running time (denoted as "CPU"). Here, IT and CPU represents the arithmetic mean of the number of iterations and running time required to  each algorithms to reach the prescribed tolerance $10^{-4}$, and these values are replicated $10$ times. \\

For the equation \eqref{eq:eq1}, the tensor $\fm A$ is randomly generated using PyTorch, and the right-hand side $\fm B$ is set to be $\fm B= \fm A * \fm X_\ast + \fm \epsilon$, where $\fm X_\ast$ is the least-square solution of \eqref{eq:eq-1} and the noise term.  The initial guess is given $\x{0} = \fm O$ and $\fm Z^{(0)}=\fm B$. All the  computations stop when the relative error ($err_k$) at the $k-$th iteration satisfies $err_k < 10^{-5}$ or when $IT$ exceeds $2000$ steps. The relative error of the solution is represented by the following
\begin{align*}
    err_k = \frac{\Vert \x{k}-\fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2}{\Vert \fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2}
\end{align*}
where $\fm X_\ast = \fm A^\dagger * \fm B$ is generated using PyTorch. For the noise $\epsilon$, it is computing like in \cite{huang2024TREK} as follows
\begin{align}
    \epsilon = a  \zeta  \frac{\Vert \beta\Vert_F} {\Vert \zeta \Vert_F}
\end{align}

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:concl}
This work presents and extends the greedy double block extended Kaczmarz method from matrix to tensor  systems for solving large-scale linear systems of tensor equations under the t=product.  The convergence guarantee is given and  several numerical examples illustrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
