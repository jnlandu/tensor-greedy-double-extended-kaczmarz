\begin{abstract} 

The randomized Kaczmarz method is a widely adopted iterative method for solving  linear systems of equations. To solve large-scale inconsistent linear systems of tensor equations under the t-product, we propose a tensor greedy double block extended Kaczmarz (TGDBEK) method. We prove  theoretically the convergence guarantees and showed that the proposed method converges linearly to the minimum-norm least-square solution of the tensor system. Moreover, we assess the performance of the proposed method through several numerical experiments. Compared to existing methods, the TGDBEK does not require predefined partitions of the tensor system and reduces the running time for solving large inconsistent system and requires less iteration.

\vspace{.5mm} 

{\bf Keywords}. Tensor equations, randomized extended Kaczmarz, tensor greedy randomized Kaczmarz,  t-product.

\section{Introduction}
Third-order tensors provide a natural representation for multiway data such as colour images, hyperspectral scenes, and multi-contrast medical volumes. In many imaging and reconstruction tasks the forward model is a \emph{t-linear} operator acting on a tensor unknown through the t-product algebra proposed by Kilmer and collaborators~\cite{kilmer2011facto, kilmer2013thirdOrder}. The t-product retains much of the familiar matrix calculus — transpose, orthogonal projectors, pseudoinverse — while preserving the multiway structure that is lost under naive vectorisation, and has found applications in tensor neural networks, compressed sensing, and image recovery~\cite{zhang2018, qi2021, wang2020, newman2018, miao2020gener}. This paper focuses on large-scale, possibly inconsistent, t-linear systems of the form
\begin{align}
\label{eq:eq1}
\mathcal{A} * \mathcal{X} \approx \mathcal{B}, \qquad
\mathcal{A}\in\mathbb{R}^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3},\quad
\mathcal{B}\in\mathbb{R}^{N_1\times K\times N_3},\quad
\mathcal{X}\in\mathbb{R}^{N_2\times K\times N_3},
\end{align}
where $*$ denotes the t-product and $\mathcal{B} = \overline{\mathcal{B}} + \varepsilon$ is contaminated by noise $\varepsilon$. Inconsistency is the rule in practice — noise, modelling error, and undersampling all perturb the data — so the natural target is the minimum-norm least-squares solution $\mathcal{X}_\ast = \mathcal{A}^\dagger * \mathcal{B}$. Representative applications include colour image deblurring and MRI reconstruction with multiple acquisition channels.

For matrix systems, Kaczmarz-type row-action methods are attractive for large, sparse operators because each iteration touches only a small portion of the system. The randomised Kaczmarz (RK) method selects rows proportionally to their squared norms and converges linearly in expectation for consistent systems~\cite{strohmer2009}. To handle inconsistency, Zouzias and Freris~\cite{zouzias2013rando} introduced the randomised extended Kaczmarz (REK), which adds an auxiliary variable that drives the residual toward $\ker(A^\top)$, yielding linear convergence to the minimum-norm least-squares solution. Two further accelerations have been studied: (i) \emph{block sampling}, which replaces single-row projections with block projections proportional to the Frobenius norm~\cite{necoara2019faste, du2020rando, tondji2023faster}, and (ii) \emph{greedy selection}, which restricts the candidate rows to those with the largest normalised residual at each step, often reducing the iteration count substantially~\cite{bai2018greed, bai2019greed, bai2021greed}.

These ideas have recently been extended to the t-product setting. Ma and Molitor~\cite{molitor2022} proposed the tensor randomised Kaczmarz (TRK) and showed it is equivalent to block randomised Kaczmarz in the Fourier domain, making it superior to methods applied to vectorised tensors. The tensor randomised block Kaczmarz (TRBK)~\cite{ba02020TRB} further improves accuracy, while~\cite{chen2021, wang2022, wang2023, lia2024, zhang2024} study Kaczmarz-type methods for tensor recovery and completion. To handle inconsistency, Huang et al.~\cite{huang2023TREK} proposed TREK and its block variant TREBK, along with a greedy variant TREGBK. A pseudoinverse-free alternative, TREABK, was introduced in~\cite{lian2024TREABK}. A limitation shared by TRBK, TREBK, and TREGBK is that they rely on \emph{predefined, fixed partitions} of the row and column index sets, which can interact unfavourably with heterogeneous sparsity patterns and make the convergence rate sensitive to partition quality.

Inspired by the greedy block extended Kaczmarz framework for matrices~\cite{su2025}, we propose the \emph{Tensor Greedy Double Block Extended Kaczmarz} (TGDBEK) method. TGDBEK eliminates fixed partitions by dynamically building active sets of row and column slices at each iteration through greedy thresholding of normalised residual scores. An auxiliary tensor $\mathcal{Z}$ tracks the component of $\mathcal{B}$ outside the range of $\mathcal{A}$, so the $x$-update acts on a progressively cleaned right-hand side. The contributions of this paper are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We propose TGDBEK, a partition-free greedy double extended Kaczmarz scheme in the t-product setting, with a self-contained algorithmic description that mirrors the structure of the matrix REK family.
    \item We establish linear convergence to the minimum-norm least-squares solution under standard assumptions, using projector geometry and t-linear algebra identities.
    \item We demonstrate performance on colour image deblurring and on sparse tensor systems from SuiteSparse~\cite{suiteSparse}, comparing against TREK, TREBK, and TREGBK.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:prelim} introduces notation and t-product preliminaries. Section~\ref{sec:tdgbek} describes the TGDBEK algorithm and establishes the convergence guarantee. Numerical experiments are in Section~\ref{sec:numexp}. Section~\ref{sec:concl} draws conclusions.

   
\section{Notations and preliminaries} \label{sec:prelim}
In this section , we discuss about the notations and the properties of tensor using the t-product that are  used in the sequel.
\subsection{Basic notation}
Throughout the paper,  the calligraphic letters such $\fm A, \fm B$ are used  to denote tensors. For an integer $m\geq 1$, we denote $[m]:=\{1, \ldots, m \}$. For a three-order tensor $\fm A$  $\fm A_{ijk}$ denotes the $(i, j,k)^th$ element of $\fm A$. $\fm A_{i, :, :}$, $\fm A_{:, j, :}$ and $\fm A_{:, :, k}$ are used to denote the horizontal slices, the lateral slices, and the frontal slices of the tensor $\fm A$, respectively. The pseudo-inverse of $\fm A$ is denoted as $\fm A^\dagger$ and the minimum and the maximum non zero singular values of a matrix are denoted as $\sigma_{\min}(\cdot)$ and $\sigma_{\max}(\cdot)$, respectively.


\subsection{Tensor preliminaries}
In this part, we provide  a brief review of main  definitions and results related to tensor using the t-product that are used in the sequel.  We use the notations from \cite{kilmer2011facto, kilmer2013thirdOrder}.\\
For a third-order tensor $\fm A\in \r^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$, let $\m A_i = \fm A_{:, :, i}$. We define the block circulant matrix  $\bcirc(\fm A)$  of $\fm A$ as ,
$$
    \bcirc(\mcala):=\bem\mbf A_1 &\mbf A_{N_3} & \cdots & \mbf A_2\\ \mbf A_2 & \mbf A_1 & \cdots &\mbf A_3\\ \vdots &\vdots & \ddots &\vdots\\ \mbf A_{N_3}& \mbf A_{N_3-1}& \cdots &\mbf A_1\eem\in\mbbr^{N_1N_3\times N_2N_3}.
$$
Moreover, we define the operations $\fold(\cdot)$  and its inverse unfold($\cdot$) as follows
$$
\unfold(\mcala):=\bem\mbf A_1\\ \mbf A_2\\ \vdots\\ \mbf A_{N_3}\eem\in\mbbr^{N_1N_3\times N_2},\qquad \fold\left(\bem\mbf A_1\\ \mbf A_2\\ \vdots\\ \mbf A_{N_3}\eem\right):=\mcala.
$$ 

\begin{definition}[t-product, \cite{kilmer2011facto, kilmer2013thirdOrder}]
For $\mcala\in\mbbr^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$ and $\mcalb\in\mbbr^{N_2\times K\times N_3}$, the {\it t-product} between $\mcala$ and $\mcalb $ is  a $ N_1\times K\times N_3$ tensor  defined and denoted  $\mcala*\mcalb$ 
$$
\mcala*\mcalb:=\fold(\bcirc(\mcala)\unfold(\mcalb)).
$$	
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[\cite{kilmer2011facto, kilmer2013thirdOrder}]
Let $\mcala \in \r^{N_1\times \times N_2 \times N_3}$ and $\mcalb \in \r^{N_2\times K\times N_3}$. Then the t-product between $\fm A $ and $\fm B$ satisfy the following properties
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The separability in the first dimension, that is 
    \begin{align*}
        \left( \fm A * \fm B\right)(i, :, :) = \fm A(i, :, :) * \fm B.
    \end{align*}
    \item  The component form decomposition on the second dimension
    \begin{align}
        \fm A * \fm B = \sum_{j=1}^{N_2} \fm A(:, j, :) * \fm B (j, :, :).
    \end{align}
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[identity tensor]
 The identity tensor $\mcali\in\mbbr^{N\times N\times N_3}$ is the tensor whose first frontal slice is the $N\times N$ identity matrix, and whose other frontal slices are all zeros.	
\end{definition}
For $\mcala\in\mbbr^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$ and $\mcali\in\mbbr^{N_1\times N_1\times N_3}$, it holds that \beq\label{ii}\mcala_{i,:,:}=\mcali_{i,:,:}*\mcala.\eeq For $\mcala\in\mbbr^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$ and $\mcali\in\mbbr^{N_2\times N_2\times N_3}$, it holds that \beq\label{ij}\mcala_{:,j,:}=\mcala*\mcali_{:,j,:}.\eeq

\begin{definition}[transpose]
The transpose of $\mcala\in\mbbr^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$, denoted by $\mcala^\top$, is the $N_2\times N_1\times N_3$ tensor obtained by transposing each of the frontal slices and then reversing the order of transposed frontal slices $2$ through $N_3$.
\end{definition} 
For $\mcala\in\mbbr^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$, we have  \beq\label{ttop}\bcirc(\mcala^\top)=(\bcirc(\mcala))^\top.\eeq %and \beq\label{eqj}\mcala*\mcala^\top=\sum_{j=1}^{N_2}\mcala_{:,j,:}*(\mcala_{:,j,:})^\top.\eeq 
   
\begin{definition}[identity tensor]
 The identity tensor $\mcali\in\mbbr^{N\times N\times N_3}$ is the tensor whose first frontal slice is the $N\times N$ identity matrix, and whose other frontal slices are all zeros.	
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[inner product]
The inner product between $\mcala$ and $\mcalb$ in $\mbbr^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$ is defined as $$\la\mcala,\mcalb\ra:=\sum_{i,j,k}\mcala_{ijk}\mcalb_{ijk}.$$ 
\end{definition}

For $\mcala\in\mbbr^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$, $\mcalb\in\mbbr^{N_2\times K\times N_3}$, and $\mcalc\in\mbbr^{N_1\times K\times N_3}$, it holds that \beq\label{tran}\la\mcala*\mcalb,\mcalc\ra=\la\mcalb,\mcala^\top*\mcalc\ra.\eeq

We will intensively employ the Frobenius norm of tensors to measure the distance between tensors. We  provide the spectral norm, Frobenius norms under the t-product.
\begin{definition}  For $\fm A \in \r^{N_1 \times N_2 \times N_3}$, its spectral norm and the Frobenius norm are defined as follows
\begin{align}
    \Vert \fm A \Vert_2 &  := \Vert \bcirc (\fm A ) \Vert_2\\
    \Vert \fm A \Vert_F &:= \sqrt{\langle \fm A , \fm A \rangle} = \sqrt{\sum_{i,j,k}}\fm A(i,j,k)^2
\end{align}
\end{definition}
\begin{lemma}
    For $\fm A\in \r^{N_1\times N_2 \times N_3}$ and $\fm B\in \r^{N_1\times N_2 \times N_3}$, it holds that 
    \begin{align}
        \Vert \fm A * \fm B \Vert_F\leq \Vert \fm A \Vert_2 \Vert \fm B \Vert_F
    \end{align}
\end{lemma}
\begin{definition} For $\fm A\in \r^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$, its pseudoinverse, denoted by $\fm A^\dagger$, is the $N_2\times N_1\times N_3$ tensor satisfying
\begin{align}
    \bcirc(\fm A^\dagger) = \bcirc(\fm A )^\dagger
\end{align}
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:lem-10}
For $\fm A\in \r^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$ and  $\fm B\in \r^{N_2\times K\times N_3}$, the following holds
\begin{align}
\fm A^\top * \fm A * \fm A^\dagger = \fm A^\top * \fm B
\end{align}
\end{lemma}
\begin{definition}[\cite{an2024randomized}] For $\fm A\in \r^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$, the range  and the null of of $\fm A$ are defined as 
\begin{align*}
    \text{range}(\fm A ) &:= \left\{\fm A * \fm Y\; :\; \fm Y\in \r^{N_2\times K\times N_3} \right\}\\
    \text{null}(\fm A) &:= \left\{ \fm X \in \r^{N_2\times K \times N_3 }\;:\; \fm A * \fm X = 0    \right\}
\end{align*}
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:lem-12}
For $\fm A\in \r^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$ and $\fm X\in $ range($\fm A$),  the following holds 
\begin{align}
    \Vert \fm A^\top * \fm X \Vert_F^2 &  \geq \sigma_{\min}^2(\bcirc(\fm A))\Vert \fm X \Vert_F^2
\end{align}
\end{lemma}

\section{The tensor greedy double block extended Kaczmarz algorithm}\label{sec:tdgbek}

Note that the range($\fm A $) and null($\fm A$) are orthogonal spaces to each other. For the the system  \eqref{eq:eq1}, $\fm B \notin$ range($\fm A $). Therefore, there must exist $\fm Z \in $null($\fm A$) such that $\fm B= (\fm B - \fm Z )  + \fm Z $, where $\fm B- \fm Z \in $ range($\fm A$). Solving the system \eqref{eq:eq1}  amounts to solving separately $\fm A^\top * \fm Z = \fm O$ and $\fm A* \fm X = \fm B - \fm Z$. Following the REK method \cite{zouzias2013rando}, it suffices to doubly apply randomized Kaczmarz Kaczmarz method to both equations. The following algorithm solves the equation eq. ~\eqref{eq:eq1}.

% According to the sum decomposition, $\fm A^\top * \fm Z =\fm O$ can be written as a


\refstepcounter{algorithm}%
\label{alg:alg-1}%
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular*}{160mm}{l}
\toprule {\bf Algorithm 1:} Tensor Greedy Double Block Extended Kaczmarz (TGDBEK)\\
\hline \noalign{\smallskip}
\quad {\bf Input}: $\mathcal{A}\in\mathbb{R}^{N_1\times N_2\times N_3}$, $\fm B\in\mathbb{R}^{N_1\times K\times N_3}$, iterations $\ell$, parameter $\eta\in(0,1]$.\\
\noalign{\smallskip}
\quad {\bf Initialize}: $\mathcal{X}^{(0)}=\mathcal{O}$ and $\mathcal{Z}^{(0)}=\fm B$.\\
\noalign{\smallskip}
\quad {\bf for} $k=0,1,\ldots,\ell-1$ {\bf do}\\
\noalign{\smallskip}
% \quad \qquad {\it (Z-step: push $\mathcal{Z}$ toward $\mathrm{Null}(\mathcal{A}^\top)$)}\\ \noalign{\smallskip}
\quad \qquad $\displaystyle \varepsilon_k^{z}=\eta\max_{1\le j\le N_2}\left\{\frac{\big\|(\mathcal{A}_{:,j,:})^\top * \mathcal{Z}^{(k)}\big\|_{\mathrm{F}}^2}{\big\|\mathcal{A}_{:,j,:}\big\|_{\mathrm{F}}^2}\right\}$\\ \noalign{\smallskip}
\quad \qquad $\displaystyle U_k=\left\{\, j\in[N_2] : \big\|(\mathcal{A}_{:,j,:})^\top * \mathcal{Z}^{(k)}\big\|_{\mathrm{F}}^2 \ge \varepsilon_k^{z} \big\|\mathcal{A}_{:,j,:}\big\|_{\mathrm{F}}^2 \right\}$\\ \noalign{\smallskip}
\quad \qquad Update $\displaystyle \mathcal{Z}^{(k+1)}=\mathcal{Z}^{(k)}-\mathcal{A}_{:,U_k,:}*(\mathcal{A}_{:,U_k,:})^\dagger*\mathcal{Z}^{(k)}$\\
\noalign{\smallskip}
\quad \qquad $\displaystyle \varepsilon_k^{x}=\eta\max_{1\le i\le N_1}\left\{\frac{\big\|\fm B_{i, :, :}-\mathcal{Z}^{(k+1)}_{i, :, :}-\mathcal{A}_{i, :, :}*\mathcal{X}^{(k)}\big\|_{\mathrm{F}}^2}{\big\|\mathcal{A}_{i,:,:}\big\|_{\mathrm{F}}^2}\right\}$\\ \noalign{\smallskip}
\quad \qquad $\displaystyle J_k=\{\, i\in[N_1] : \big\|\fm B_{i, :, :}-\mathcal{Z}^{(k+1)}_{i, :, :}-\mathcal{A}_{i, :, :}*\mathcal{X}^{(k)}\big\|_{\mathrm{F}}^2\ge \varepsilon_k^{x}\,\Vert \ai{i} \Vert_F^2\}$\\ \noalign{\smallskip}
\quad \qquad Update $\displaystyle 
\mathcal{X}^{(k+1)}=\mathcal{X}^{(k)}+(\fm A (J_k, :, :))^\dagger*
\Big(\fm B_{J_k,:,:}-\mathcal{Z}^{(k+1)}_{J_k,:,:}-\fm A_{J_k, :, :}*\mathcal{X}^{(k)}\Big)$\\
\noalign{\smallskip}
\quad {\bf end}\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular*}
\end{center}
At each iteration, the TGDBEK method constructs the active sets $U_k$ and $J_K$ for both columns and rows slices, which it uses in place blocks to projects the current iterates onto. This makes the TGDBEK independent from any  predefined partitions of  the tensor system.

\begin{theorem}
Assume the system \eqref{eq:eq1} is inconsistent and let
$\fm B^\perp := \fm B - \fm A * \fm A^\dagger * \fm B$
denote the component of $\fm B$ orthogonal to $\mathrm{range}(\fm A)$.
Let $J = \bigcup_{l=0}^{k} J_l$ be the union of all active row-index sets up to step $k$,
and let $\|\fm A_{:,\overline{U},:}\|_F^2 = \max_{l \leq k}\|\fm A_{:,U_l,:}\|_F^2$
be the largest active column-block norm seen up to step $k$.
The tensor sequence $\{\fm X^{(k)}\}$ generated by Algorithm~\ref{alg:alg-1}
converges to the minimum-norm least-squares solution $\fm X_\ast = \fm A^\dagger * \fm B$.
Moreover, for all $k \geq 0$,
\begin{align}
    \Vert \fm X^{(k+1)} - \fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2
    \;\leq\;
    \alpha^{k+1}\Vert \fm X^{(0)} - \fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2
    \;+\;
    \gamma\,\frac{\alpha^{k+1}-\beta^{k+1}}{\alpha - \beta}
    \cdot\beta
    \,\Vert \fm B - \fm B^\perp \Vert_F^2,
\end{align}
where
\begin{align*}
\alpha &= 1 - \theta\,
    \frac{\sigma_{\min}^2\!\left(\bcirc(\fm A_{J,:,:})\right)}
         {\sigma_{\max}^2\!\left(\fm A_{J,:,:}\right)},
\qquad
\beta  = 1 - \eta\,
    \frac{\sigma_{\min}^2\!\left(\bcirc(\fm A^\top)\right)}
         {\|\fm A\|_F^2 - \|\fm A_{:,\overline{U},:}\|_F^2},
\qquad
\gamma = \frac{1}{\sigma_{\min}^2\!\left(\fm A_{J_k,:,:}\right)},
\end{align*}
$\theta = \min_{l \leq k}\theta_l$ with
$\theta_l = \|\fm X^{(l)} - \fm X_\ast\|^2_{\mathrm{range}((\fm A_{J_l,:,:})^\top)}\big/
            \|\fm X^{(l)} - \fm X_\ast\|_F^2 \in (0,1]$,
and $\eta \in (0,1]$ is the greedy threshold parameter of Algorithm~\ref{alg:alg-1}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[Sufficient conditions for $\alpha,\beta\in(0,1)$]
\label{rem:alpha-beta}
The bound above implies $\|\fm X^{(k)}-\fm X_\ast\|_F^2\to 0$ as $k\to\infty$
provided $\alpha,\beta\in(0,1)$, which holds under the following conditions.

\textit{Upper bounds.}
$\alpha<1$ holds whenever $\theta>0$ and
$\sigma_{\min}^2\!\left(\bcirc(\fm A_{J,:,:})\right)>0$, i.e., the accumulated
row-block $\fm A_{J,:,:}$ has full column rank in the t-product sense.
$\beta<1$ holds whenever $\eta>0$,
$\sigma_{\min}^2\!\left(\bcirc(\fm A^\top)\right)>0$ ($\fm A$ has full column rank),
and $\|\fm A_{:,\overline{U},:}\|_F^2<\|\fm A\|_F^2$ (the active column-block
never exhausts the full Frobenius norm of $\fm A$).

\textit{Lower bounds.}
$\alpha\geq 0$ always, since $\theta\leq 1$ and
$\sigma_{\min}^2\!\left(\bcirc(\fm A_{J,:,:})\right)\leq
\sigma_{\max}^2\!\left(\fm A_{J,:,:}\right)$ make the subtracted term at most $1$.
For $\beta$, the $z$-step is an orthogonal projection, so its true contraction
ratio $C_k = \|\left(\fm A_{:,U_k,:}^\top\right)^\dagger *\fm A_{:,U_k,:}^\top *
(\fm Z^{(k)}-\fm B^\perp)\|_F^2\,\big/\,\|\fm Z^{(k)}-\fm B^\perp\|_F^2$
lies in $[0,1]$.
The proof establishes
$C_k\geq\eta\,\sigma_{\min}^2\!\left(\bcirc(\fm A^\top)\right)\big/
\bigl(\|\fm A\|_F^2-\|\fm A_{:,\overline{U},:}\|_F^2\bigr)$,
giving $\beta = 1-\eta\,\sigma_{\min}^2\!\left(\bcirc(\fm A^\top)\right)\big/
\bigl(\|\fm A\|_F^2-\|\fm A_{:,\overline{U},:}\|_F^2\bigr)\leq 1-C_k\leq 1$.
Since projections are contractions, $1-C_k\geq 0$, and hence $\beta\geq 0$.
\end{remark}

\begin{proof}
From Lemma \ref{lem:lem-10}, we obtain 
\begin{align*}
    \fm A^\top *\fm B^\perp  =\fm A^\top  *\fm B - \fm A^\top * \fm A * \fm A^\dagger * \fm B =\fm A ^\top * \fm B  - \fm A ^\top * \fm B = 0
\end{align*}
In particular, for any $U_k \subset [N_2]$   we get $\fm A_{:, U_k, :}^\top *\fm B^\perp = 0$.
Now, using the fact $\fm A * \fm A^\dagger = \left(\fm A * \fm  A^\dagger \right)^\top = (\fm A^\dagger)^\top * \fm A ^\top = (\fm A^\top )^\dagger * \fm A^\top   $  and the $z$-update in Alg. \ref{alg:alg-1}, we can get
\begin{align*}
    \fm Z^{(k+1)} - \fm B^\perp  & = \left(\fm Z^{(k)} - \fm B^\perp\right) - \left(\fm A^\top_{:, {U_k}, :} \right)^\dagger* \fm A^\top_{:, U_k, :} *\left( \fm Z ^{(k)}-\fm B^\perp \right)\\
    & = \left(\fm I_{:, U_k, :} - \left( \a{U_k}^\top\right) ^\dagger \a{U_k}^\top\right)*\left( \fm Z^{(k)}- \fm B^\perp \right).
\end{align*}
Since $\left( \a{U_k}^\top\right) ^\dagger \a{U_k}^\top$  is a $t-$ tensor projector,  the Pythagorean theorem helps to obtain
\begin{align}
    \Vert   \fm Z^{(k+1)} - \fm B^\perp  \Vert_F^2  = \Vert  \fm Z^{(k)} - \fm B^\perp  \Vert_F^2 - \Vert  \left(\fm A^\top_{:, {U_k}, :} \right)^\dagger* \fm A^\top_{:, U_k, :} *\left( \fm Z ^{(k)}-\fm B^\perp \right) \Vert_F^2. \label{eq:eq-1b}
\end{align}
Moreover, from the Lemma \ref{lem:lem-12}, we  note that 
\begin{align}
    \Vert \left(\a{U_k}^\top \right)^\dagger * \a{U_k}^\top *\left(\fm Z^{(k)}-\fm B^\perp \right) \Vert_F^2 & =  \Vert \left(\a{U_k}^\top \right)^\dagger * \a{U_k}^\top *\fm Z^{(k)}\Vert_F^2 \notag\\
    & \geq \sigma_{\min}^2((\a{U_k}^\top) ^\dagger)\Vert \a{U_k}^\top * \fm Z^{(k)} \Vert_F^2 
  \quad \text{(by Lemma \ref{lem:lem-12})} \notag\\
    & = \sigma_{\min}^2(\bcirc(\a{U_k}^\top) ^\dagger) \sum_{j\in U_k} \Vert \a j ^\top * \fm Z^{(k)} \Vert_F^2 \notag\\
    & \geq \sigma_{\min}^2(\bcirc(\a{U_k}^\top) ^\dagger) \epsilon^z_k \Vert \a{U_k} \Vert_F^2 \notag\\
    & =  \frac{\Vert \a{U_k} \Vert_F^2}{\sigma_{\max}^2(\bcirc(\a{U_k}^\top))} \epsilon_k^z .\label{eq:eq-2}
\end{align}
Now,  by the help of Lemma \ref{lem:lem-10}, we observe that
\begin{align*}
    \a{U_{k-1}}^\top \fm Z^{(k)} & = \a{U_{k-1}}^\top \left( \fm Z^{(k-1)} - \a{U_{k-1}}*\a{U_{k-1}}^\dagger \fm Z^{k-1} \right)\\
    & =  \a{U_{k-1}}^\top*\fm Z^{(k-1)} - \a{U_{k-1}}^\top *\a{U_{k-1}}\a{U_{k-1}}^\dagger* \fm Z^{k-1} = \fm O, 
\end{align*}
so that for all $k=0, 1, \ldots$, we can get
\begin{align*}
    \Vert \fm A^\top * \fm Z^{(k)}\Vert_F^2  & = \sum_{j \in U_k} \Vert \a j ^\top  * \fm Z^{(k)} \Vert_F^2 + \sum_{j\in U^c_{k-1}}\Vert \a j ^\top  * \fm Z^{(k)} \Vert_F^2 \\
    &=  \sum_{j\in U^c_{k-1}}\Vert \a j ^\top  * \fm Z^{(k)} \Vert_F^2  =  \sum_{j\in U^c_{k-1}} \frac{ \Vert \a j ^\top  * \fm Z^{(k)} \Vert_F^2}{ \Vert \a j \Vert_F^2}\Vert \a j \Vert_F^2\\
    & \leq \max_{j \in [N_2]}\left\{  \frac{ \Vert \a j ^\top  * \fm Z^{(k)} \Vert_F^2}{ \Vert \a j \Vert_F^2} \right\}\left(\Vert \fm A \Vert_F^2 - \Vert \a{U_k} \Vert_F^2 \right).
\end{align*}
Hence
\begin{align}
    \epsilon^z_k = \eta \max_{j \in [N_2]}\left\{  \frac{ \Vert \a j ^\top  * \fm Z^{(k)} \Vert_F^2}{ \Vert \a j \Vert_F^2} \right\} &\geq \eta \frac{ \Vert \fm A^\top \fm Z^{(k)}\Vert_F^2}{\Vert \fm A \Vert_F^2 - \Vert \a{U_k} \Vert_F^2} \notag\\
    & \geq \eta\frac{ \sigma_{\min}^2(\bcirc(\fm A^\top)) \Vert \fm Z^{(k)}-\fm B^\perp \Vert_F^2}{\Vert \fm A \Vert_F^2 - \Vert \a{U_k} \Vert_F^2},  \label{eq:eq-3}
\end{align}
where in the last inequality we used $ \Vert \fm A^\top *  \fm Z^{(k)}\Vert_F^2 =  \Vert \fm A^\top * \left( \fm Z^{(k)} - \fm B^\perp \right)\Vert_F^2 \geq \sigma_{\min}^2(\bcirc(\fm A^\top)) \Vert \fm Z^{(k)}-\fm B^\perp \Vert_F^2 $.\\
Substitute \eqref{eq:eq-3} in \eqref{eq:eq-2} we can obtain
\begin{align}
     \Vert \left(\a{U_k}^\top \right)^\dagger * \a{U_k}^\top *\left(\fm Z^{(k)}-\fm B^\perp \right) \Vert_F^2 & \geq \frac{\eta \Vert \a{U_k} \Vert_F^2}{\Vert \fm A \Vert_F^2 - \Vert \a{U_k} \Vert_F^2} \frac{\sigma_{\min}^2(\bcirc(\fm A^\top)) \Vert \fm Z^{(k)}-\fm B^\perp \Vert_F^2}{\sigma_{\max}^2(\bcirc(\a{U_k}^\top))}. \label{eq:eq-4}
\end{align}
Let $\Vert \a{\overline{U}} \Vert_F^2 := \max_k \{ \Vert \a{U_k}\Vert_F^2\}$ so that, combining \eqref{eq:eq-1b} and  \eqref{eq:eq-4} above, we can finally obtain the upper bound
\begin{align}
     \Vert   \fm Z^{(k+1)} - \fm B^\perp  \Vert_F^2  & \leq  \left( 1 - \frac{\eta \Vert \a{U_k} \Vert_F^2}{ \sigma_{\max}^2(\bcirc(\a{U_k}^\top)) } \frac{\sigma_{\min}^2(\bcirc(\fm A^\top))}{\Vert \fm A \Vert_F^2 - \Vert \a{\overline{U}} \Vert_F^2}   \right) \Vert  \fm Z^{(k)} - \fm B^\perp \Vert_F^2  \notag \\
     & \leq \left( 1 - \eta  \frac{\sigma_{\min}^2(\bcirc(\fm A^\top))}{\Vert \fm A \Vert_F^2 - \Vert \a{\overline{U}} \Vert_F^2}   \right) \Vert  \fm Z^{(k)} - \fm B^\perp \Vert_F^2 .
\end{align}
where  in the last inequality we used the fact that $\frac{\Vert \a{U_k}\Vert_F^2 }{\sigma_{\max}^2(\bcirc(\a{U_k}))}\geq 1$. The fact that  we can decompose $\b{J_k} = \ai{J_k}*\fm X^\ast + \bp{J_k} $, we can rewrite the the $x-$ update as,
\begin{align*}
   \fm X^{(k+1)} = \x k - \ai{J_k}^\dagger * \left( \a{J_k} *\left(\x k - \fm X^\ast \right) + \left(\z{k+1}{J_k} - \bp{J_k}  \right)\right)
\end{align*}
Subtracting $\fm X_\ast$  both sides  in the equality above, we obtain
\begin{align}
   \x {k+1} - \fm X_\ast  & =  \x{k} - \fm X_\ast  - \ai{J_k}^\dagger * \left( \a{J_k} *\left(\x k - \fm X^\ast \right) + \left(\z{k+1}{J_k} - \bp{J_k}  \right)\right) \notag\\
   & =  \left( \fm I - \ai{J_k}^\dagger *  \a{J_k} \right) *\left(\x k - \fm X^\ast   \right) +  \ai{J_k}^\dagger* \left(\z{k+1}{J_k} - \bp{J_k}  \right)
\end{align}
Again $\a{J_k}^\dagger \a{J_k}$ is a $t-$ orthogonal projector  (slice-wise  orthogonal in the Fourier  domain), hence the two terms are orthogonal in the Frobenius norm. Therefore,  from the  Pythagorean theorem , we can obtain
\begin{align}
    \Vert \x{k+1} - \fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2 = \Vert \left( \fm I - \ai{J_k}^\dagger *  \a{J_k} \right) *\left(\x k - \fm X^\ast   \right) \Vert_F^2  +  \Vert  \ai{J_k}^\dagger* \left(\z{k+1}{J_k} - \bp{J_k}  \right) \Vert_F^2  \label{eq:eq-5}
\end{align}
Let  $\x{k}-\fm X_\ast = \left( \x{k} - \fm X_\ast \right)_{\text{R}((\a{J_k})^\top)} + \left(\x k - \fm X_\ast \right)_{\text{R}((\a{J_k})^\top)^\perp }$ and $\Vert \left( \x{k} - \fm X_\ast \right)_{\text{R}((\a{J_k})^\top)} \Vert_F^2  = \theta_k \Vert \x k - \fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2$. Then, using the Pythagorean theorem and the  Lemma (), we get :
% \scriptsize
\begin{align}
\Vert \left( \fm I - \ai{J_k}^\dagger *  \a{J_k} \right) *\left(\x k - \fm X^\ast   \right) \Vert_F^2  & = \Vert \x k - \fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2 - \Vert  \ai{J_k}^\dagger *  \a{J_k}  *\left(\x k - \fm X^\ast   \right) \Vert_F^2  \notag\\
& \leq  \Vert \x k - \fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2  \notag\\
& -\sigma_{\min}^2(\a{J_k}^\dagger) \sigma_{\min}^2(\a{J_k}) \Vert\left( \x{k} - \fm X_\ast \right)_{\text{R}((\a{J_k})^\top)} \Vert_F^2 \notag\\
& = \left( 1 - \theta_k \frac{\sigma_{\min}^2(\bcirc(\a{J_k}))}{\sigma^2_{\max}(\a{J_k})}\right) \Vert \x k - \fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2 \notag  \\
& \leq \left( 1 - \theta \frac{\sigma_{\min}^2(\bcirc(\a{J}))}{\sigma^2_{\max}(\a{J})}\right) \Vert \x k - \fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2 \label{eq:eq-6}, 
\end{align}
where we denote $J = \{J_0, 1, \ldots, J_k \}$ and $\theta:= \min_{k} \{\theta_k \}$, and $\a{J}$ the stacked or the union block of tensors  $\a{J_k}$. For the second term,  we first note that $\Vert \fm A^\dagger * \fm X \Vert_F \leq \Vert \fm A ^\dagger \Vert_2^2 \Vert \fm X \Vert_F^2$. Then, we obtain
\begin{align}
  \Vert  \ai{J_k}^\dagger* \left(\z{k+1}{J_k} - \bp{J_k}  \right) \Vert_F^2  & \leq \sigma_{\max}^2((\a{J_k})^\dagger) \Vert  \z{k+1}{J_k} - \bp{J_k} \Vert_F^2 \notag\\
  & = \frac{1}{\sigma_{\min}^2(\a{J_k})} \Vert  \z{k+1}{J_k} - \bp{J_k} \Vert_F^2.
  \label{eq:eq-7}
\end{align}
It results from  \eqref{eq:eq-5}, \eqref{eq:eq-6}, and \eqref{eq:eq-7} that
\begin{align*}
 & \Vert \x{k+1} - \fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2 \\
 &  \leq \left( 1 - \theta \frac{\sigma_{\min}^2(\bcirc(\a{J}))}{\sigma^2_{\max}(\a{J})}\right) \Vert \x k - \fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2 +  \frac{1}{\sigma_{\min}^2(\a{J_k})} \Vert  \z{k+1}{J_k} - \bp{J_k} \Vert_F^2\\
 & \leq \left( 1 - \theta \frac{\sigma_{\min}^2(\bcirc(\a{J}))}{\sigma^2_{\max}(\a{J})}\right) \Vert \x k - \fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2 + \frac{1}{\sigma_{\min}^2(\a{J_k})} \left( 1 - \eta  \frac{\sigma_{\min}^2(\bcirc(\fm A^\top))}{\Vert \fm A \Vert_F^2 - \Vert \a{\overline{U}} \Vert_F^2}   \right) \Vert  \fm Z^{(k)} - \fm B^\perp \Vert_F^2 .
\end{align*}
To ease the notation, let $\alpha =  1 - \theta \frac{\sigma_{\min}^2(\bcirc(\a{J}))}{\sigma^2_{\max}(\a{J})}$, $\beta = 1 - \eta  \frac{\sigma_{\min}^2(\bcirc(\fm A^\top))}{\Vert \fm A \Vert_F^2 - \Vert \a{\overline{U}} \Vert_F^2}  $, \; $\gamma =\frac{1}{\sigma_{\min}^2(\a{J_k})}$. Then, induction over $k$ yields
\begin{align*}
    \Vert \x{k+1} - \fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2  & \leq \alpha  \Vert \x k - \fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2 + \gamma\beta \Vert \fm Z^{(k)} - \fm B^\perp \Vert_F^2\\
    & \leq \alpha \left( \alpha  \Vert \x k - \fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2 + \gamma\beta \Vert \fm Z^{(k)} - \fm B^\perp \Vert_F^2 \right) + \gamma\beta^2 \Vert \fm Z^{(k-1)} - \fm B^\perp \Vert_F^2\\
    &\vdots\\
    & \leq \alpha^{k+1} \Vert \x 0 - \fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2  + \gamma\sum_{l=0}^k \alpha^{l} \beta^{k-l+1}  \Vert \fm Z^{(k-l-1)} - \fm B^\perp \Vert_F^2\\
    & \leq \alpha^{k+1} \Vert \x 0 - \fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2  + \gamma\Vert \fm B_{R(\fm A)}\Vert_F^2 \sum_{l=0}^k \alpha^{l} \beta^{k-l+1}\\
    &\leq \alpha^{k+1} \Vert \x 0 - \fm X_\ast \Vert_F^2 + \gamma\frac{\alpha^{k+1} -\beta^{k+1}}{\alpha - \beta} \beta\,\Vert \fm B_{R(\fm A)}\Vert_F^2
\end{align*}
\end{proof}

\section{Numerical experiments} \label{sec:numexp}
In this section, numerical experiments are conducted to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed TGDBEK algorithm and to compare its performance against the state-of-the-art methods TREK, TREBK, and TREGBK~\cite{huang2023TREK} as well as TREABK~\cite{su2025}. All experiments were implemented in Python using PyTorch~\cite{ansel2025pytorch} and executed on Google Colab as well as an Apple MacBook Pro with an M3 chip. The complete code is publicly available at \url{https://github.com/jnlandu/tensor-greedy-double-extended-kaczmarz}.

For a fair comparison, we use the number of iteration steps (IT) and the wall-clock running time in seconds (CPU) as the primary performance metrics. Both quantities are reported as arithmetic means over $10$ independent trials. For the block-partitioned methods (TREBK, TREGBK), the blocks are generated sequentially following~\cite{du2020rando}. The initial iterates are set to $\fm X^{(0)} = \fm O$ and $\fm Z^{(0)} = \fm B$. All algorithms are terminated when the relative squared error (RSE) satisfies
\begin{align*}
    \mathrm{RSE}_k = \frac{\bigl\|\fm X^{(k)} - \fm X_\ast\bigr\|_F^2}{\|\fm X_\ast\|_F^2} < 10^{-5},
\end{align*}
or when the iteration count exceeds a prescribed maximum. Here $\fm X_\ast = \fm A^\dagger * \fm B$ denotes the minimum-norm least-squares solution computed via the tensor pseudo-inverse. The additive noise tensor is generated following~\cite{huang2023TREK} as
\begin{align}
    \fm\epsilon = a\,\fm\zeta\,\frac{\|\fm B\|_F}{\|\fm\zeta\|_F}, \label{eq:noise}
\end{align}
where $\fm\zeta$ is a standard Gaussian tensor and $a > 0$ controls the noise level.

Table~\ref{tab:ex2} reveals two distinct performance regimes. For overdetermined sparse tensors with many more row slices than column slices (\texttt{nos5}, \texttt{ash85}, \texttt{Cities}), TGDBEK reduces the iteration count by a factor of $2$--$4$ relative to the best block baseline, consistent with the greedy selection concentrating projection effort on the most informative constraints at each step. \end{remark}

\begin{example}[\textbf{Effect of the greedy threshold $\eta$}] \label{ex:eta}
The parameter $\eta \in (0,1]$ controls the selectivity of the greedy block rule: row block $i$ is added to the active set $U_k$ if and only if $\|\fm A_{i,:,:}^\top * \fm r^{(k)}\|_F^2 \geq \eta \cdot \max_j \|\fm A_{j,:,:}^\top * \fm r^{(k)}\|_F^2$, and similarly for column blocks. A small $\eta$ includes many blocks per step (akin to TREBK), whereas $\eta \to 1$ retains only the single most informative block (akin to TREK). We study the sensitivity of TGDBEK to this parameter on the dense system $\fm A \in \mathbb{R}^{500\times20\times10}$ with noise level $a=10^{-2}$, maximum $2000$ iterations, and tolerance $10^{-6}$, averaged over $20$ independent runs.

Table~\ref{tab:eta} and Fig.~\ref{fig:eta} report the average iteration count and CPU time as functions of $\eta$. For $\eta \leq 0.5$, TGDBEK selects a generous number of blocks at each step: convergence is achieved in very few iterations ($\leq 25$) and the total wall time remains below $0.1\,\mathrm{s}$. As $\eta$ increases beyond $0.5$, the active set shrinks, per-step progress decreases, and both IT and CPU grow substantially. At $\eta = 1.0$ the algorithm degenerates to a TREK-like single-block update, requiring approximately $350$ iterations and more than $4\,\mathrm{s}$. These results confirm that the optimal range for $\eta$ lies in $[0.1, 0.6]$; we adopt $\eta = 0.6$ as the default in all other experiments as a conservative choice that still retains strong performance while selecting a meaningfully greedy subset of blocks.

\begin{remark}
The sharp increase in CPU time between $\eta=0.5$ and $\eta=0.6$ (Table~\ref{tab:eta}) reflects a qualitative change in the active-set size: below $\eta\approx0.5$ the greedy criterion consistently selects a large fraction of the available blocks, enabling bulk progress per step that offsets the overhead of evaluating all block residuals; above $\eta=0.5$ the active set contracts rapidly, and the iteration cost grows as progressively fewer blocks contribute to each update. The practical recommendation is therefore to keep $\eta$ in the range $[0.3, 0.6]$, where both IT and CPU are near-minimal. For problems where per-step cost is the bottleneck (e.g.\ very large tensors), a smaller $\eta$ may be preferable; for problems where iteration overhead dominates, values closer to $0.5$--$0.6$ offer the best trade-off.
\end{remark}

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:concl}
We have proposed the Tensor Greedy Double Block Extended Kaczmarz (TGDBEK) method for solving large-scale, possibly inconsistent, linear tensor systems under the t-product framework. At each iteration, TGDBEK selects the most informative row block and column block via a greedy maximum-residual criterion, and updates both the solution and the constraint correction simultaneously. A linear convergence guarantee is established, and the convergence rate is shown to depend on the spectral properties of the system tensor.

Numerical experiments on dense overdetermined systems, sparse SuiteSparse tensors, and image deblurring benchmarks consistently demonstrate the advantage of the greedy selection strategy. TGDBEK attains lower final residuals and reaches a prescribed accuracy in significantly fewer iterations than the competing methods TREK, TREBK, TREGBK, and TREABK; on the color flower recovery task, for instance, it achieves the same reconstruction quality as TREGBK in $37\%$ fewer iterations. For the high-iteration dense benchmark, TGDBEK is the only method that reaches RSE $< 10^{-5}$. In the gray image deblurring experiment with a Shepp--Logan phantom, it also yields the best PSNR and SSIM values. An analysis of the greedy threshold parameter $\eta$ shows that values in the range $[0.2, 0.5]$ offer the best balance between convergence speed and per-iteration cost.

These results suggest that TGDBEK is a robust and competitive solver for tensor least-squares problems arising in imaging and data science. Future work may explore adaptive selection of $\eta$, randomized block strategies, and extensions to higher-order tensors.
